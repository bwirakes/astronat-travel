Most people think astrology is personal. But for centuries, the most rigorous branch of the tradition has been turned outward — tracking the astrology of nations, places, and collective turning points. Geodetic astrology and mundane astrology are where celestial mechanics meets geopolitics, economics, and the lived conditions of a location.

Here is what these disciplines actually are, how they work together, and why they matter for anyone serious about locational astrology.

What is Geodetic Astrology?

01 / 04

A zodiac fixed to the globe

Geodetic astrology is a system in which 0° Aries is permanently anchored to the Greenwich Meridian (0° longitude). From there, each degree of the tropical zodiac corresponds to one degree of geographical longitude. The result is a static planetary map of the world — a kind of collective natal chart baked into the earth itself.

The technique gives every location on the planet its own geodetic Ascendant and Midheaven — fixed reference points that describe the inherent character and exposure of a place. Unlike astrocartography, which shifts with your birth chart, the geodetic chart belongs to the location, not to you.

When outer planets transit a location's geodetic angles, the conditions of that place tend to shift in ways that align with the planetary archetype. Pluto crossing a country's geodetic Midheaven has historically correlated with governance crises, power restructuring, and moments of collective reckoning. Saturn with consolidation, austerity, or institutional pressure. Jupiter with expansion, growth, and increased visibility on the world stage.

Geodetic equivalents

The geodetic equivalent of a planet in your natal chart is the longitude on earth where that planet "falls" in the geodetic system. It is one of the most underused tools in locational astrology — revealing places where you are likely to feel a particular planetary energy more acutely, simply because the geography resonates with your chart.

A natal Venus at 15° Taurus, for example, has its geodetic equivalent along approximately 45° East longitude — a line running through parts of East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and into Russia. People with strong Venus placements often describe feeling unusually creative, socially activated, or relationship-oriented when they are near these longitudes.

What is Mundane Astrology?

03 / 04

The astrology of the collective

Mundane astrology is the branch of astrology concerned with nations, governments, natural phenomena, economic cycles, wars, pandemics, and collective cultural shifts. It is the oldest strand of the tradition — practiced by Babylonian, Hellenistic, Arabic, and Renaissance astrologers long before personal natal astrology became dominant.

Where natal astrology asks "what does this mean for me?", mundane astrology asks "what does this mean for us?" — for a city, a country, a generation, a civilisation. Its tools are the charts of nations (often cast for independence moments, constitutional signings, or founding events), ingress charts cast for capitals and key cities, lunation cycles, eclipse paths, and — most powerfully — the great outer planet cycles.

The Saturn–Jupiter conjunction cycle (~20 years) is foundational: it has long been observed to correlate with shifts in economic paradigms and political leadership. The Saturn–Pluto cycle (~35 years) with periods of structural breakdown and hard consolidation. The Pluto ingresses into new signs bring generational-scale transformations visible in hindsight across many centuries of recorded history.

Mundane astrology does not claim to predict specific events. It identifies archetypal pressures, timing windows, and thematic signatures. The actual events that manifest depend on the specific national chart, the historical context, and the broader ecosystem of transits active at the same time.

  • ♃♄
    Jupiter–Saturn Conjunction (~20 yrs)
    The "great chronocrator" cycle. Historically correlated with political regime changes, economic paradigm shifts, and collective reorientation toward new institutions and ideologies.
  • ♄♇

    Associated with periods of structural hard reset — austerity, authoritarian pressure, and the dismantling of what is no longer sustainable. Followed eventually by regeneration.
  • ♆♇

    The longest observable outer planet cycle. Correlated with civilisation-level transformations: Renaissance, Industrial Revolution, and the emergence of entirely new cultural paradigms.
  • ☊
    Eclipse Cycles & Saros Series
    Eclipses activate the nodal axis and the eclipse degree for months. When an eclipse falls on a nation's Ascendant, Sun, or ruler's planet, events tied to that archetype tend to crystallise in the following months.
  • ♈
    Aries Ingress & Seasonal Charts
    The Sun's entry into Aries marks the astrological new year. A chart cast for this moment at the capital of any nation functions as a forecast chart for the coming twelve months — one of mundane astrology's most consistently applied tools.

Geodetic + Mundane:
The Full Picture

Geodetic astrology and mundane astrology are most powerful when used together. Geodetic techniques tell you how a location is inherently wired. Mundane cycles tell you when those wires are being activated. Together, they let you understand not just where to be — but when the timing for a particular place is most significant.

  1. 1.
    Establish the geodetic angles for your location — its Ascendant, Midheaven, and what planets currently occupy those angles geodetically.
  2. 2.
    Overlay the mundane cycle — identify which outer planet cycles are currently activating the nation's chart, and when the next ingress or eclipse falls near these geodetic points.
  3. 3.
    Check your personal chart against both layers — where do your natal planets align with the geodetic angles and current mundane transits? This three-way convergence is where individual experience and collective events intersect most directly.
  4. 4.
    Track over time — mundane cycles unfold slowly. A single reading captures a moment; sustained attention to the cycles reveals the full arc of meaning for a location and its relationship to you.

What is the difference between geodetic astrology and astrocartography?

Astrocartography (ACG) is a personal system: it projects your natal planets onto the globe based on where each planet was rising, setting, culminating, or at the nadir at the moment of your birth. The lines shift depending on your birth chart. Geodetic astrology is impersonal and fixed — it assigns zodiac degrees to longitudes based on a permanent rule (0° Aries = Greenwich), regardless of when you were born. ACG shows where your chart is most activated; geodetics shows the inherent character of a location, plus where your natal planets happen to land in that fixed system. Used together, they are considerably more powerful than either alone.

Is geodetic astrology actually accurate? What does the evidence say?

This is the right question to ask. The honest answer is: the evidence is suggestive but not definitive. There is a significant body of case studies — many published in research journals like Correlation — showing striking correlations between outer planet transits to geodetic angles and major collective events. Researchers like Chris McRae and others have documented historical examples that are difficult to dismiss as mere coincidence. However, geodetic astrology has not been subjected to the kind of rigorous double-blind statistical testing that would satisfy peer-reviewed scientific standards. My position is that it is a tool worth using seriously, with appropriate epistemic humility — treating it as a heuristic framework for pattern recognition rather than a deterministic oracle.

What exactly is a mundane chart, and how is it different from a natal chart?

A natal chart is cast for a specific person at the moment of their birth. A mundane chart is cast for a collective entity or event — a nation (typically at the moment of independence or constitutional founding), a city, an institution, or a significant collective moment (such as a planetary ingress, eclipse, or lunation). The chart is then treated analogously to a natal chart: the Ascendant describes the entity's public presentation, the Midheaven its reputation and leadership, the 8th house shared resources and crisis, and so on. Transits and progressions to these charts are used to understand collective timing and cycles.

How do I find my geodetic equivalent positions?

The calculation is straightforward. Take any natal planet's tropical zodiac degree. Convert that degree to a raw number from 0–360 (0° Aries = 0, 0° Taurus = 30, etc.). The geodetic equivalent longitude is simply that number minus 180 (to convert from the Aries-anchored system to standard East/West longitude). The resulting figure gives you the longitude on earth where that planet has its geodetic resonance. For example: a natal Venus at 15° Taurus = 45° in the geodetic system = 45° East longitude — a line running through Ethiopia, Saudi Arabia, and western Russia. A reading with me will map all your significant planets onto the globe and explain what each longitude band tends to activate for you personally.

Can mundane astrology predict future events?

Astrology — including mundane astrology — does not predict specific events. What it identifies are archetypal pressures, timing windows of heightened sensitivity, and thematic patterns that tend to correlate with certain types of collective experience. Whether a Saturn–Pluto transit to a nation's Sun manifests as an economic crisis, a political upheaval, a natural disaster, or a slow structural reform depends on an enormous range of factors that no astrological technique can specify in advance. What mundane astrology offers is a framework for understanding why certain periods feel the way they do — and for identifying windows in which particular kinds of collective action or experience are more or less likely to gain traction.

Do I need an accurate birth time for geodetic work?

For the core geodetic equivalent technique (mapping your planets to longitudes), you do not need an accurate birth time — only your birth date and approximate year. The slow-moving planets (Jupiter through Pluto) move so slowly that a day's margin of error is usually irrelevant for longitude calculations. For faster-moving planets like the Moon, an approximate time (within a few hours) is helpful. Where birth time matters more is when we incorporate your natal Ascendant and Midheaven into the analysis — those angles shift significantly within a single day. In such cases, I can work with a birth time range, or we can set aside the angles and focus on planetary positions alone.

How does geodetic astrology work alongside relocation astrology and ACG?

Think of it as three complementary lenses. Astrocartography shows your personal planetary lines — where your chart is most activated. Geodetic equivalents show the inherent longitudinal resonance of your natal planets — locations that carry those planetary themes at a collective, earth-level frequency. Relocation astrology recalculates your chart as if you were born at a new location, revealing how your house cusps and planetary house placements shift when you move. Using all three together gives a remarkably rich picture: where you are most personally energised (ACG), where you resonate collectively (geodetics), and how your psychological landscape reshapes itself when you arrive (relocation). A full Astrocartography Deep Dive reading typically incorporates elements of all three.
